We will prove that the arithmetic progression \(\equiv 8 \pmod{21}\) contains infinitely many primes. Equivalently, we will see that there are infinitely many primes of the form \(21n+8\), \(n\geqslant0\). For this purpose, consider the polynomial \[f(x):=x^{6} + 2624 x^{5} + 2869189 x^{4} + 1673379728 x^{3} + 549025263424 x^{2} + 96078803512082 x + 7006306553612521.\]

The main Theorem

To prove that there exist infinitely many primes \(\equiv 8 \pmod{21}\) we can proceed by contradiction. Suppose there are finitely many primes \(\equiv 8\pmod{21}\) and denote them by \(p_1, p_2,\dots,p_m\). Since \(29 \equiv 8 \pmod{21}\), we can write the list as \(29, p_{2},p_{3},\dots, p_m\) (so \(p_{1}=29\)). Now, let \(Q:=2 \cdot 7 \cdot 71 \cdot 281 \cdot 491 \cdot 8779 \cdot 78305641\cdot p_{2}p_{3}\cdots p_m\). Consider the following congruence equation system: \[\begin{cases} c\equiv 1\pmod{29^2}\\ c\equiv 0\pmod{21Q}. \end{cases}\] The Chinese Remainder Theorem guarantees the existence of \(c\in\mathbb{Z}\) that is a solution to the above system since \(29\) does not divide \(21Q\). It follows that \[\begin{gathered} f(c)\equiv f(1)=7102936058639569\equiv 29\cdot20\pmod{29^2},\\ f(c)\equiv f(0)=4789 \cdot 6427 \cdot 227633407\pmod{21Q}. \end{gathered}\]

In particular, observe that the prime \(p_{1}=29\) divides \(f(c)\), but \(29^2\) does not.

Lemma 1. Every prime that divides \(f(c)\) is \(\equiv 1\pmod{21}\) \((\)except for \(p_{1}=29)\).

Proof. Let \(r\) be a prime divisor of \(f(c)\) different from \(29\). In Section 2 we will establish that \(r=2, 3, 7, 71, 281, 491, 8779, 78305641\) or \(r\equiv 1,8\pmod{21}\). For now, we will assume this is true. To reach a contradiction, suppose \(r\not\equiv 1\pmod{21}\). Thus, \(r\equiv 8\pmod{21}\) or \(r=2, 3, 7, 71, 281, 491, 8779, 78305641\), so \(r\) divides \(21\cdot2 \cdot 7 \cdot 71 \cdot 281 \cdot 491 \cdot 8779 \cdot 78305641\cdot p_{2}p_{3}\cdots p_m=21Q\). Since \(f(c)\equiv 4789 \cdot 6427 \cdot 227633407\pmod{21Q}\) and \(r\) is a divisor of \(21Q\), we deduce that \(f(c)\equiv 4789 \cdot 6427 \cdot 227633407\pmod{r}\). But \(r\) is a divisor of \(f(c)\), so \(f(c)\equiv 0 \pmod{r}\). Therefore, \(4789 \cdot 6427 \cdot 227633407\equiv 0\pmod{r}\). Thus, it must happen that \(r=4789, 6427, 227633407\), which are \(\equiv 1 \pmod{21}\). This forces \(r\) to be \(\equiv 1 \pmod{21}\), a contradiction. Therefore, \(f(c)\) is only divisible by primes \(\equiv 1\pmod{21}\) (and by \(p_{1}=29\)). ◻

Finally, from the fact that \(f(c)\) has every prime divisor \(\equiv 1 \pmod{21}\) except for \(p_{1}=29\) it follows, mod \(21\), that \(f(c)=1\cdot1\cdots 1\cdot8=8\) (note that \(8\) only appears once because \(p_{1}=29\equiv8\pmod{21}\) and the fact that \(29^2\) does not divide \(f(c)\)). However, observe that \(f(c)\equiv f(0)\equiv 1\pmod{21}\). This is a contradiction. Therefore, the arithmetic progression \(\equiv 8\pmod{21}\) contains infinitely many primes.

Properties of the polynomial \(f(x)\)

To complete the proof of the main Theorem in Section 1 we must justify that every prime divisor \(p\) of \(f(c)\) either belongs to the finite set \[T:=\{2, 3, 7, 71, 281, 491, 8779, 78305641\}\] or satisfies \(p\equiv 1,8\pmod{21}\). To see this, we must first recall the expression of the discriminant of a polynomial.

Definition 2. The discriminant of a monic polynomial \(A(x)=x^m+a_{m-1}x^{m-1}+\cdots+a_1x+a_0\) is given, in terms of its roots \(\{r_1,r_2,\dots,r_m\}\subset\mathbb{C}\) (not necessarily distinct), by \[\label{eq:discrim} \Delta(A)=\prod_{i<j}(r_i-r_j)^2, \quad 1\leqslant i,j\leqslant m.\]

It will be useful to remember that the \(21\)st cyclotomic polynomial is \(\Phi_{21}(x)=x^{12} - x^{11} + x^{9} - x^{8} + x^{6} - x^{4} + x^{3} - x + 1\). We shall also define what a prime divisor of a given polynomial is.

Definition 3. Let \(A(x)\in\mathbb{Z}[x]\) be a polynomial. We say that a prime number \(p\) is a prime divisor of \(A\) (or simply that \(p\) divides \(A\)) if there exists \(m\in\mathbb{Z}\) such that \(p\) divides \(A(m)\).

With the definition above, we are interested in describing the prime divisors of \(f\).

Let’s now start the proof. Consider the set \(S:=\{1, 2, 4, 5, 10, 13\}\) and the values \(h(\zeta^{s}):=(\zeta^{s}-21)(21-\zeta^{8s})\), with \(s\in S\) and \(\zeta:=e^{2\pi i/{21}}\), a \(21\)st primitive root of unity (thus a root of \(\Phi_{21}(x)\)). A simple calculation shows that \(f(x)\) can be written as \[f(x)=\prod_{s\in S}\big(x-h(\zeta^{s})\big)=x^{6} + 2624 x^{5} + 2869189 x^{4} + 1673379728 x^{3} + 549025263424 x^{2} + 96078803512082 x + 7006306553612521.\] The discriminant of \(f(x)\) can be calculated1 to be \(\Delta(f)=-1 \cdot 2^{6} \cdot 7^{5} \cdot 71^{2} \cdot 281^{2} \cdot 491^{2} \cdot 8779^{2} \cdot 78305641^{2}\).

Now, suppose that \(p\) is a prime divisor of \(f\) such that \(p\notin T\). Next, consider a field \(\mathbb{F}\) containing both the finite field \(\mathbb{F}_p\) and \(\zeta\)2. Since \(p\) divides \(f\), working in \(\mathbb{F}\), there exists \(a\in\mathbb{Z}\) such that \[f(a)=\prod_{s'\in S}\big(a-h(\zeta^{s'})\big)=0.\] Since \(\mathbb{F}\) is a field, there exists some \(s\in S\) such that \(a=h(\zeta^{s})\).

Lemma 4. The equality \(h(\zeta^s)=h(\zeta^{ps})\) holds in \(\mathbb{F}\).

Proof. Observe that the following calculation holds in \(\mathbb{F}\): \[\begin{aligned} \label{eq:reproots} h(\zeta^{s})&=a\notag \\ &=a^p\notag \\ &=h(\zeta^{s})^p\notag \\ &=(\zeta^{s}-21)^p(21-\zeta^{8s})^p\notag \\ &=(\zeta^{ps}-21^p)(21^p-\zeta^{8ps})\notag \\ &=(\zeta^{ps}-21)(21-\zeta^{8ps})\notag \\ &=h(\zeta^{ps}), \end{aligned}\] where we have used Fermat’s little theorem in the second equality. The fifth equality, on the other hand, relies on the fact that \(\mathbb{F}\) has characteristic \(p\) (so that \((c+d)^p=c^p+d^p\) for every \(c,d \in \mathbb{F}\)) and the following one, on Fermat’s little theorem. ◻

Therefore, equality \(h(\zeta^{s})=h(\zeta^{ps})\) means that \(h(\zeta^{ps})=h(\zeta^{s})\) is a root of \(\overline{f(x)}\in\mathbb{F}[x]\).

Lemma 5. \(h(\zeta^{ps})\) is also a root of \(f(x)\) in \(\mathbb{Q}(\zeta)\) \((\)the smallest subfield of \(\mathbb{C}\) containing \(\zeta)\).

Proof. Begin by noting that the value \(h(\zeta^{ps})\) only depends on the value of \(ps \bmod{21}\) since it only appears as an exponent of \(\zeta\). Since \(p\) does not divide \(21\) and \(s\) is coprime to \(21\), \(ps\) is coprime to \(21\) (so \(ps \bmod{21}\) is coprime to \(21\)) and hence \(\zeta^{ps}\) is a primitive \(21\)st root of unity.

There are now only two options: either \(ps \bmod{21}\in S\) or \(ps \bmod{21}\notin S\). In the first case, \(h(\zeta^{ps})\) is a root of \(f(x)\), observing the expression of \(f(x)\). In the latter case, note that every integer \(ps \bmod{21}\) relatively prime to \(21\) not in \(S\) satisfies \(ps\equiv 8t\pmod{21}\) for some \(t\in S\) (for instance, if \(ps \bmod{21}=19\), pick \(t=5\in S\) so that \(19\equiv 8\cdot5 \pmod{21}\)). This means that \(h(\zeta^{ps})=h(\zeta^{8t})\). Let us prove that \(h(\zeta^{8t})=h(\zeta^{t})\), so \(h(\zeta^{ps})=h(\zeta^{8t})=h(\zeta^{t})\) is also a root of \(f(x)\). Indeed, \[\begin{aligned} h(\zeta^{8t})&=(\zeta^{8t}-21)(21-\zeta^{8^2t})\\ &=(\zeta^{8^2t}-21)(21-\zeta^{8t})\\ &=(\zeta^{t}-21)(21-\zeta^{8t})\\ &=h(\zeta^{t}), \end{aligned}\] where we have used that \(\zeta^{8^2t}\) only depends on the value of \(8^2t \bmod{21}\) and the fact that \(8^2\equiv 1\pmod{21}\). Therefore, \(h(\zeta^{ps})=h(\zeta^{t})\) is always a root of \(f(x)\) in \(\mathbb{Q}(\zeta)\). ◻

Lemma 6. \(h(\zeta^{ps})\) and \(h(\zeta^{s})\) are the same root of \(f(x)\) in \(\mathbb{Q}(\zeta)\).

Proof. If \(h(\zeta^{ps})\) and \(h(\zeta^{s})\) were two distinct roots of \(f(x)\) in \(\mathbb{Q}(\zeta)\), we know because of the equality \(h(\zeta^{s})=h(\zeta^{ps})\) that they would be the same in \(\mathbb{F}\). Therefore, observing the expression of the discriminant, it follows that \(\Delta(f \bmod{p})=\Delta(f) \bmod{p}=0\), so \(p\) divides \(\Delta(f)=-1 \cdot 2^{6} \cdot 7^{5} \cdot 71^{2} \cdot 281^{2} \cdot 491^{2} \cdot 8779^{2} \cdot 78305641^{2}\). This is a contradiction with our choice of \(p\). Thus, \(h(\zeta^{ps})\) and \(h(\zeta^{s})\) are in fact the same root of \(f(x)\) in \(\mathbb{Q}(\zeta)\). ◻

Therefore, the equality \[h(\zeta^{ps})=\big(\zeta^{ps}-21\big)\big({21}-\zeta^{8ps}\big)=\big(\zeta^{s}-21\big)\big({21}-\zeta^{8s}\big)=h(\zeta^{s})\] holds in \(\mathbb{Q}(\zeta)\). Next, write the above equation in terms of \(\theta:=\zeta^{s}\) and multiply both sides by \(-1\). This changes yield \[\begin{aligned} 441-{21}\big(\theta^{p}+\theta^{8p}\big)+\theta^{(1+8)p}&=441-{21}\big(\theta+\theta^{8}\big)+\theta^{1+8}, \notag \\ -21\big(\theta^{p}+\theta^{8p}\big)+\theta^{9p}&=-21\big(\theta+\theta^{8}\big)+\theta^{9}. \quad \quad (1) \end{aligned}\] The right-hand side of the equation above does not depend on \(p\). The left-hand side only depends on the value of \(p\bmod{21}\), since \(p\) only appears as an exponent of \(\theta\). The above equality gives information about \(p\), which is what we are interested in.

Lemma 7. The fact that \((1)\) holds implies that \(p \bmod{21}\in H:=\{1,8\}\).

Proof. To prove this, we will check every value of \(p\) such that \(p \bmod{21}\notin H\) and conclude that \((1)\) is not true in \(\mathbb{Q}(\theta)\) in those cases. Therefore, we shall see the following: if \(p \bmod{21}\in G\setminus H=\{2, 4, 5, 10, 11, 13, 16, 17, 19, 20\}\), then \(-h(\theta^p)\neq -h(\theta)\). This will automatically imply what we want to prove: since \((1)\) holds, \(p \bmod{21}\in H\). To see this, rewrite \((1)\) as \[\label{eq:equalityQ} -21\big(\theta^{p}+\theta^{8p}\big)+\theta^{9p}+21\big(\theta+\theta^{8}\big)-\theta^{9}=0\] and trade \(\theta\) for \(x\), since the condition in the above equality in \(\mathbb{Q}(\theta)\) is equivalent to the condition \[\label{eq:polycheck0} -21\big(x^p+x^{8p}\big)+x^{9p}+21\big(x+x^{8}\big)-x^{9}=0 \quad \quad (2)\] in \(\mathbb{Q}[x]/(\Phi_{21}(x))\cong\mathbb{Q}(\theta)\) (\(\Phi_{21}(x)\) is also the minimal polynomial of \(\theta\), since \(\theta=\zeta^{s}\) is a primitive \(21\)st root of unity). We will explicitly write the case \(p=2 \bmod{21}\) (the remaining values of \(p \bmod{21}\in G\setminus H\) are left as an exercise to the reader). With this value of \(p\), equation \((2)\) becomes \[\begin{aligned} A(x)&:=-21\big(x^{2}+x^{16}\big)+x^{18}+21\big(x+x^{8}\big)-x^{9}\\ &=x^{18} - 21 x^{16} - x^{9} + 21 x^{8} - 21 x^{2} + 21 x=0. \end{aligned}\]

If we recall that \(\mathbb{Q}(\theta)\cong\mathbb{Q}[x]/(\Phi_{21}(x))\), the above equation is equivalent to \(A(x)\) being a multiple of the \(21\)st cyclotomic polynomial, \(\Phi_{21}(x) = x^{12} - x^{11} + x^{9} - x^{8} + x^{6} - x^{4} + x^{3} - x + 1\). Therefore, we are interested in showing that the residue \(R(x)\) of the division \(A(x)/\Phi_{21}(x)\) satisfies \(R(x)\neq 0\), from which our result will follow. A simple Euclidean division of polynomials shows that \(A(x)=B(x)\cdot\Phi_{21}(x)+(-x^{11} + 20 x^{9} + 21 x^{8} - x^{4} + 21 x)\), with \(B(x)\) a polynomial of degree \(6\), so \(R(x)=-x^{11} + 20 x^{9} + 21 x^{8} - x^{4} + 21 x \neq 0\). Therefore, equality \((1)\) implies that \(p \bmod{21} \in H\), that is, \(p\equiv 1,8\pmod{21}\). ◻

In conclusion, every prime divisor \(p\) of \(f(c)\) either belongs to the finite set \[T:=\{2, 3, 7, 71, 281, 491, 8779, 78305641\}\] or satisfies \(p\equiv 1,8\pmod{21}\), which finally settles the main Theorem in Section 1.
